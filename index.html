Restaurants

Sōwadō
Izakaya Hiroo · Hiroo H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Behind a featureless concrete wall with a plain black sliding door, there's no sign. That's the point. Chef Hideaki Sakai — originally from Fukuoka — runs this as the sibling restaurant to Sakai Shokai, channeling Kyushu ingredients into refined small plates over charcoal. The genshiyaki (ancient-style skewers grilled slowly over a brazier) anchor the experience, but the tsukune dipped in golden egg yolk and the rotating sashimi are what people talk about afterward. Excellent natural wine and sake list. Omakase is the move. Reservations required — try TableCheck.
Sakai Shokai
Izakaya Shibuya · Shibuya GZF ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
The original restaurant from the same Chef Hideaki Sakai. A dimly lit wooden counter, an open fire, and a 7-course omakase built on Kyushu ingredients and French-inflected technique. The ham cutlet is legendary — thick, crispy, and universally cited as a standout. You sit down, you tell them to feed you, and they do. Strong natural wine program and a sake list curated with the same care as the food. Five-minute walk from Shibuya Station. Reservations essential.
Goryū Kubo
Kaiseki Nishi-Azabu · Roppongi HE ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Two Michelin stars within seven months of opening — and Chef Takeshi Kubo seems unbothered by the attention. A purist who favors substance over spectacle, Kubo sources directly from producers across Japan: wild soft-shell turtle in spring, saury in autumn, matsuba crab in winter. The philosophy is simple — perfect ingredients, meticulous technique, minimal interference. Recently relocated to an elegant new space on the 3rd floor of the Shalom building in Nishi-Azabu (1-13-14), with private counter seating and a more refined atmosphere than the original basement location. Extremely difficult to book.
Pizza Marumo
Pizza Ebisu · Ebisu H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Chef Yuki Motokura trained at Savoy, then opened his own place in late 2022 and promptly landed in the global top 10 pizzerias (Best Chef Awards, 2023). He calls his pies "Tokyo-style" — the dough has a mochi-like chew from a 24-hour ferment, baked in a custom wood-fired oven built by a traditional Japanese kiln maker at 500°C. The margherita is textbook, but the Japanese Umami pizza (shiitake cream, mozzarella, pecorino, mackerel, bonito flakes, kombu, soy sauce) is the signature everyone orders. Reservations via their website — book early, especially evenings and weekends.
400°C (Yonhyakudo)
Pizza Kagurazaka · Kagurazaka T ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
A 12-seat counter in the charming Kagurazaka neighborhood that has held the #1 pizza ranking on Tabelog. The interior feels more gallery than restaurant — minimalist, hushed, precise. A gas conveyor belt oven produces a remarkably thin, crisp, airy crust. The FNT pizza (whipped mascarpone, blue cheese, honey) is the signature and genuinely unlike anything else. Bib Gourmand recognized. The reservation system is notoriously strict: narrow booking windows, 70-minute seatings, minimum order of one pizza and one drink per person. Multiple locations now, but the Kagurazaka original is the one.
Ginza Shinohara
Kaiseki Ginza · Ginza GMH ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Chef Takesho Shinohara ran one of the most revered kaiseki restaurants in rural Shiga Prefecture, consistently ranked in Japan's top three on Tabelog. In 2016, he walked away from it all to start fresh in Ginza. The cooking is rooted in the powerful flavors of rural Japan — bold, seasonal, heartfelt — presented in an elegant basement space with an L-shaped white-wood counter. The hassun (seasonal platter) changes monthly and is the emotional centerpiece of the meal. Tabelog Gold. Reservations open on the 25th of each month and go fast.
Ushimatsu
Yakiniku Nishi-Azabu · Hiroo H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
A yakiniku restaurant for people who take beef seriously. Chef Tatsuro Hirakubo purchases entire female Tajima-breed black wagyu cattle — purebred, 30+ months old — and breaks them down in-house to build a coursed experience that's closer to omakase than typical grill-your-own. The signature "shibari tan" (tongue wrapped around chopped scallions) went viral before the restaurant even opened. Somm-curated wine list of 90+ bottles. The hidden sub-basement counter "Uramatsu" (7 seats, four nights a week) is the unicorn reservation.
Yoroniku Tokyo
Yakiniku Azabudai Hills · Kamiyacho H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
The Azabudai Hills outpost of the celebrated Yoroniku brand — arguably Tokyo's most fashion-forward yakiniku. Chef Hideyuki Kuwahara approaches wagyu with what he calls a "DJ remix spirit," blending influences from sushi and French cooking into a meat kaiseki format that feels nothing like a typical grill restaurant. The signature meat bruschetta opens most meals; the thinly sliced chateaubriand grilled rare over low heat and the sukiyaki with truffle aroma and whipped egg yolk are standouts. Ten fully private rooms with staff grilling for you — a first for a Tokyo yakiniku of this caliber. Tabelog Silver, and one of the city's toughest reservations. Closed Mondays.
Savoy
Pizza Azabu-Jūban · Azabu-Jūban NE ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
A Tokyo pizza institution since 2006, and still one of the best. The original Azabu-Jūban location has 13 counter seats facing a hulking wood-fired oven, and the menu is ruthlessly simple: margherita, marinara, and an off-menu bianca. That's it. Named after the famous Harlem jazz ballroom, there's always jazz playing. The crust is soft, chewy, and charred just right from the 500-degree oven, and the simplicity is the whole point. Walk-ins possible at lunch; reserve for dinner. A sister location, Savoy Tomato & Cheese, is a few minutes away. The newer bluefin tuna pizza at the main shop is a crowd-pleaser.
Aramaki
Yakitori Higashi-Azabu · Akabanebashi E ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Opened July 2024, and already a Michelin-recognized Inspectors' Favorite. Chef Masashi Aramaki — a former Muay Thai fighter who trained at Ginza Torishige, two-star Ginza Kojyu, and served as head chef at Yakitori Omino Kamiyacho — runs an intimate 8-seat counter. The format alternates precisely between seasonal Japanese small plates and yakitori skewers: salt-seasoned in the first half, transitioning to tare in the second, with kaiseki-caliber dishes (wanmono, hassun) woven between. Finishes with extraordinary donabe rice. One of the most exciting new restaurants in Tokyo right now.
Yakitori Omino
Yakitori Oshiage · Oshiage ZA ↗ Oshiage ↗ Kamiyacho ↗ Tabelog ↗ Tsubaki
Chef Omino Masayoshi trained at the legendary Torishiki in Meguro, then opened his own counter in an unlikely Oshiage back street in 2017. Michelin star by 2019. The philosophy: Date chicken from Fukushima, Kishu binchotan charcoal, and split-second timing. The counter forms a U around the grill, and the team calls out to each other as skewers hit their peak — salt-forward, generous cuts, served the instant they're ready. Omino Kamiyacho is the more kaiseki-influenced sibling, weaving seasonal courses between the skewers — think chicken thigh sushi, liver pâté, stuffed wings with sticky rice and chestnuts. Omino Tsubaki in Kyobashi is the newest, a tiny 7-seat basement counter where Omino-san himself now stations.
Torisho Ishii Hina
Yakitori Minami-Azabu · Azabu-Jūban NE ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
The Tokyo sibling of Osaka's Michelin-starred Torisho Ishii, this 12-seat counter in a quiet Minami-Azabu residential street takes the parent restaurant's philosophy and shifts the balance — where Osaka focuses on skewers, Hina weaves in more dishes, serving roughly one plate for every two or three sticks. The result is a yakitori omakase that feels closer to kaiseki in its pacing and ambition. Seasonal vegetables and delicate preparations sit alongside the charcoal-grilled chicken. For the closing course, four options including the signature oyakodon and a ramen built on deeply extracted chicken broth. Tabelog Bronze. Two seatings nightly (5:30pm, 8:30pm); reservations via OMAKASE only. Closed Sundays and Mondays.
Kiyota
Sushi Ginza · Ginza GMH ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Established in 1963, now helmed by fourth-generation chef Norihiko Yoshizawa. A 9-seat counter on a quiet Ginza back street where there is no menu — only omakase built from whatever the chef sourced at Toyosu that morning. The house obsession is tuna: Kiyota's relationships with fishermen and brokers are decades deep, and the maguro here is considered among the finest in Tokyo. The shari is mild, seasoned with red vinegar, and the overall approach is unfussy — the fish speaks. Tabelog Bronze, and its satellite Kiyota Hanare (designed by artist Hiroshi Sugimoto) pushes into ¥100,000+ territory.
Sushi Takeru
Sushi Ginza · Ginza GMH ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
The new generation. Takeru Watanabe trained at Ginza Kyubey and Sushi Arai before opening his own counter in Ginza 7-chome in 2022. The L-shaped yakusugi cedar counter was a gift from his mentor Yuichi Arai. The omakase opens with sashimi (the aged botan-ebi is a signature), moves through small plates including ankimo and grilled shellfish, then transitions into piece-by-piece nigiri placed directly on the counter. The tamagoyaki at the close is a production — layered with tremendous care in a large square pan. He goes to Toyosu daily, personally. Tabelog Bronze 2025.
Sushi Akira
Sushi Hiroo · Hiroo H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Named after his grandfather, Chef Kazunori Maeiwa's 8-seat counter in a quiet Hiroo apartment building has become one of Tokyo's most celebrated sushi destinations — Tabelog Silver three years running (2024–2026) with a 4.51 rating. Trained at Sushi Takumi under Keiji Nakazawa, Maeiwa blends classic Edomae technique with subtle modern touches and the culinary traditions of his native Wakayama. The omakase-only format moves through seasonal nigiri sourced daily, with the chef's deft aging and preparation letting each piece speak clearly. Reservations open exactly one month in advance and fill fast. Closed Tuesdays and Wednesdays.
Sushi Riku (鮨 陸)
Sushi Hiroo · Hiroo H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Riku Toda trained under Hachiro Mizutani at the legendary Sushi Mizutani, then honed his craft at Sugita before spending six years at top sushi counters in Bangkok. He opened his own place in Hiroo in September 2024, and it took off fast — Tabelog Award 2026 Bronze with a 4.37 rating in its first full year. The room is built around a striking 6-meter hinoki counter sourced from Mie Prefecture. Toda visits Toyosu each morning, and his omakase is classic Edomae — clean, precise, letting the fish do the talking. Service in English and Chinese. ¥40,000–50,000.
Bia (美会)
Thai-Japanese Fusion Roppongi · Roppongi HE ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
The wildcard on this list, and worth every bit of the detour. Founded by Thai epicure Beer Peragate Charoenpanich, Bia layers Thai spices and techniques over Japan's finest seasonal ingredients — the result is a kaiseki-format omakase that tastes like nothing else in Tokyo. Shark fin in suppon broth with Thai medicinal herbs. Black abalone in an oyster-omelette riff. Wagyu nam tok with caviar. The fugu is fried with Thai spices. It finishes with massaman curry, green curry, and jasmine rice. Tabelog Silver and Best New Entry. Wine pairing by a talented sommelier is strongly recommended.
Honke Abeya
Oyakodon · Chicken Marunouchi · Tokyo M ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
An Akita institution with an outpost in the basement of KITTE, directly connected to Tokyo Station. Honke Abeya is the Tokyo arm of a farm-to-table operation centered on Hinai-jidori — one of Japan's three most prized chicken breeds, raised stress-free in the northern mountains of Akita. Whole birds arrive daily and are broken down in-house. The oyakodon is the thing to order: charcoal-grilled thigh and breast over rice, finished with soft-set eggs rich enough to pick up with chopsticks, all in a dashi made from simmered Hinai chicken bones. No onions, no filler — just chicken, egg, and rice doing exactly what they should. Also worth trying: the yakitori, kiritanpo nabe (Akita rice-stick hot pot), and the local Akita craft beers. A perfect, unfussy lunch near Tokyo Station.

Bar

Gen Yamamoto
Cocktail Omakase Azabu-Jūban · Azabu-Jūban NE ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Arguably the bar that invented the omakase cocktail. Gen Yamamoto — a veteran of the New York cocktail scene who trained at David Bouley's Brushstroke — returned to Tokyo in 2012 and built an 8-seat counter from a 500-year-old slab of Japanese oak in a quiet Azabu-Jūban alley. There's no menu. You choose a 4- to 7-drink tasting set, and Yamamoto crafts each cocktail in front of you using seasonal Japanese fruit, vegetables, and fine spirits — more kitchen knife than cocktail shaker. No shaking, no ice in mixing glasses, no classics. The drinks are small, lower in alcohol, and change weekly with the seasons. The space has no music, no decoration beyond a single flower — it's closer to a tea ceremony than a bar. He speaks excellent English. Book by phone at least a week in advance; a month is safer.
Bar Centifolia
Cocktail Bar Azabu-Jūban · Azabu-Jūban NE ↗ Map
The polar opposite of Gen Yamamoto — and just around the corner in Azabu-Jūban. Where Yamamoto is monastic stillness, Centifolia is pure theater. Owner-bartender Yuzo Komai, a Suntory Cocktail Award champion, turns every drink into a performance: katana ice-carving, liquid nitrogen clouds, blue fire tracing across the bar top. There's no menu — you tell the staff your preferences (favorite fruits, flavors, spirit leanings) and Komai crafts something bespoke in front of you. Despite the showmanship, the cocktails themselves are genuinely well-made and beautifully presented. The space is small — 6th floor of a nondescript building — and reservation-only. Book via TableCheck or try DM on Instagram. Closed Wednesdays. Opens at 8:30pm.
Bar Ishinohana
Cocktail Bar Shibuya · Shibuya GZF ↗ Map
Tucked in a basement a minute from Shibuya Station, Ishinohana is the mixology bar that serious cocktail people in Tokyo point to. Owner-bartender Shinobu Ishigaki — Bacardi-Martini Grand Prix champion (2005) — works with the speed and precision of a drummer, wielding seasonal fruits, Japanese herbs, and housemade infusions to build drinks that constantly evolve. The menu spans three actual books: one for original cocktails, one dedicated entirely to 50 martini variations, and one for 50 mojitos. But the seasonal specials and Ishigaki's improvisations based on your preferences are the real draw. Try the Oribe (gin, matcha, milk, wasanbon sugar, gold powder) or just tell him what you like and let him go. The vibe is quieter and more approachable than Ginza's formal bartending temples — a proper neighborhood cocktail bar that happens to be world-class. Closed Wednesdays and Thursdays.
Eureka! (ユリーカ)
Sake Bar Nishi-Azabu · Hiroo H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Marie Chiba — sake sommelier, Sake Samurai, and the force behind the legendary Gem by Moto in Ebisu — opened her own 13-seat standing bar in Nishi-Azabu in late 2022, and it immediately became one of Tokyo's most talked-about sake destinations. There's no set menu. You tell Chiba what you're in the mood for, and she builds a sake-and-food pairing on the spot from 200+ bottles and a kitchen turning out creative small plates with French inflections. She approaches pairing scientifically, matching flavor profiles to the moment — same sake, different night, different dish. The philosophy is ichi-go ichi-e: every visit is unrepeatable. Reservations via TableCheck. Closed Mondays. ¥10,000–20,000.

Wine

LUONTO
Italian · Wine Tsukiji · Tsukiji H ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
A wine-driven Italian on a quiet Tsukiji side street, run by a caviste (cellar manager) who ages and curates every bottle himself. The name is Finnish for "nature" — a nod to the philosophy that great wine is an expression of its origins, and the restaurant should be a place to decompress and connect with that. The cellar leans heavily on wines that have hit their peak drinking window, regardless of region or style, which makes the by-the-glass selection unusually deep and interesting. The food is refined Italian built around seasonal Japanese ingredients — polenta and ragù, homemade pastas, clean preparations that let the wine lead. A proper wine dinner destination. Closed Wednesdays, Saturdays, and Sundays.
Restaurant Ararat
French · Wine Toranomon · Toranomon G ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Behind a dark, unmarked door across from a pharmacy in Toranomon, Ararat operates more like a private dining room than a restaurant. Owner-sommelier Michihito Higashihara — named Sommelier de l'Année by Le Chef magazine in 2017 — spent eight years in France and returned to Tokyo with a cellar of over 600 wines and a vision for intimate, wine-first dining. The format is coursed French bistro — think perfectly executed sirloin, silky crème caramel — served to just one or two parties per evening across five tables. The menu is kept intentionally simple so the wine can lead. Reservations required; ten seats maximum.
Goût de Jaune
Jura Wine Bar Akasaka · Roppongi-Itchome N ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Possibly the only bar in the world dedicated exclusively to wines from the Jura region of France. Sommelier Shohei Miura opened this 13-seat basement space in 2017 and has since assembled a collection of thousands of bottles, with over ten varieties of Vin Jaune available by the glass at any given time — spanning vintages from the 1950s to the present. The pairing insight is unexpected: Miura recognized shared flavor compounds between Vin Jaune and Shaoxing wine, so the food menu blends French bistro and Chinese dishes alongside Jura classics like coq au vin jaune and long-aged Comté. An obsessive, singular place. Reservations via Instagram DM. Closed Sundays.

Coffee

Leaves Coffee Roasters
Specialty Coffee Kuramae · Kuramae EA ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
Founded by Yasuo Ishii — a former professional boxer who pivoted to coffee after receiving a bag of beans as a gift in 2010. Entirely self-taught as both barista and roaster, he placed 1st in the Tokyo area and 3rd nationally at the Japan Roasting Competition in his first year competing. The philosophy is light roasts focused on clean sweetness and vibrant acidity, brewed with meticulous precision. The roastery is a minimalist, industrial space where the staff walks you through single-origin options with serious knowledge. There's also a secondary stand, Leaves Coffee Apartment, a short walk across the river in Komagata (takeaway only, paper cups).
Open Friday through Monday only, 10am–5pm. Plan accordingly.
Glitch Coffee & Roasters
Specialty Coffee Jimbocho · Jimbocho ZIS ↗ Map
The original location — not the newer Ginza outpost. Founded by Kiyokazu Suzuki, a former Paul Bassett head roaster and Japan AeroPress Champion (2014), Glitch occupies a small corner space on Chiyoda-dori in the bookish Jimbocho neighborhood. The concept is uncompromising: exclusively super-light single-origin roasts designed to maximize the fruit character inherent in great coffee. You choose from a rotating selection of beans — often a dozen or more — with the barista guiding you through flavor profiles. Pour-over is the focus (V60), with espresso also available. The "Share Roaster Project," in which Glitch lends its Probat roaster to other shops, speaks to Suzuki's community-first ethos. Expect a queue at peak hours; the space fits about 10 people. Open daily from 8am (9am weekends).
Koffee Mameya
Coffee Boutique Omotesando · Omotesando GZC ↗ Map ↗ Tabelog
More coffee consultation than café. Founded by Eiichi Kunitomo on the same site as the beloved (and now closed) Omotesando Koffee, Koffee Mameya opened in 2017 as a bean specialist — the name literally means "coffee bean store." The space is radically minimal: a small counter, three baristas, and a glass case displaying 20–30 coffees from a curated roster of Japanese and international roasters. There's no menu in the traditional sense — the barista asks what you like and prescribes the right cup, prepared as drip or espresso. No milk drinks, no seating — standing only. It's an education as much as it is a coffee. Lines can be long, especially on weekends. For a proper sit-down experience, the newer Koffee Mameya Kakeru in Kiyosumi-Shirakawa offers tasting courses and dessert pairings at a counter.

Hotels

Aman Tokyo
Luxury Hotel Otemachi · Otemachi MTCZI ↗ Map
Aman Tokyo occupies floors 33–38 of the Otemachi Tower, and the late Kerry Hill designed the interiors as a study in Japanese spatial restraint rendered at enormous scale. The lobby is the signature moment: a vast double-height hall lined in camphor wood with shoji-style screens and a ceiling inspired by traditional paper lanterns, all bathed in hushed natural light that makes you instinctively lower your voice. Rooms are the largest in the city at a minimum of 71 square meters — a borderline absurd amount of space for central Tokyo — with floor-to-ceiling windows framing either the Imperial Palace gardens or the Skytree. The spa spans two floors and includes a 30-meter pool with views over the city. The hotel that made Tokyo's luxury scene take itself seriously as a global destination.
Hoshinoya Tokyo (星のや東京)
Ryokan Hotel Otemachi · Otemachi MTCZI ↗ Map
A vertical ryokan — a genuinely strange and compelling idea, stacking the rituals of a traditional Japanese inn inside a 17-story tower two blocks from the Imperial Palace. Designed by Azuma Architect & Associates with interiors by Rie Azuma, the facade is wrapped in an asanoha (hemp leaf) pattern screen that filters light into the corridors. Each of the six floors functions as a self-contained ochanoma lounge shared by just six guest rooms, so you get that intimate ryokan atmosphere in the middle of Tokyo's financial district. The onsen on the top floor, fed by natural hot spring water from 1,500 meters below the city, is the real draw — soaking in open air while staring at skyscrapers is a disorienting pleasure. You check your shoes at the door and pad around in yukata.
Janu Tokyo (ジャヌ東京)
Luxury Hotel Azabudai Hills · Kamiyacho H ↗ Map
Aman's extroverted younger sibling — Sanskrit for "soul" — opened in late 2023 as the brand's global debut, embedded within Heatherwick Studio's Azabudai Hills development. Kerry Hill Architects designed interiors warmer and more playful than Aman's austere minimalism, with curved timber screens, terracotta tones, and deliberate social energy in the public spaces. The wellness floor is staggering: 4,000 square meters of spa, gym, pools, and treatment rooms — probably the most serious hotel wellness facility in East Asia. Rooms start at 60 square meters with a softer palette of linen, oak, and washi paper. For people who want Aman-level craft without the monastic quietude, steps from the Mori JP Tower observation deck and Olafur Eliasson installations.
The Tokyo Edition, Toranomon
Luxury Hotel Marriott Bonvoy Toranomon · Toranomon G ↗ Map
Kengo Kuma designed the interiors here, and it shows — the lobby is a moody, cavernous space with slatted wood screens and indirect lighting that feels more like entering a contemporary art gallery than a hotel. The 31st-floor Gold Bar is the real draw: an intimate, wood-paneled room with panoramic views toward Tokyo Tower that has quickly become one of the city's best-kept cocktail spots. Rooms are deliberately pared back in Kuma's signature style, all pale timber and clean lines, letting the floor-to-ceiling windows do the talking. The rooftop terrace restaurant is worth a visit even if you're not staying — open-air dining settings this central are rare in Tokyo.
Grand Hyatt Tokyo
Luxury Hotel World of Hyatt Roppongi · Roppongi HE ↗ Map
Anchored inside Roppongi Hills — Mori Building's sprawling city-within-a-city — the Grand Hyatt feels less like a hotel lobby and more like a gallery entrance, with Jun Kaneko's massive ceramic heads greeting you at the door. The 387 rooms lean into warm mahogany and stone rather than the chrome minimalism of its competitors. Ten restaurants and bars cover serious range, from The Oak Door (one of Tokyo's best hotel steakhouses) to a proper French bistro. The Nagomi Spa's red granite pool is genuinely impressive. Its location cannot be beaten for nightlife-adjacent luxury: Mori Art Museum upstairs, and Roppongi's bar scene steps away.
Andaz Tokyo
Luxury Hotel World of Hyatt Toranomon · Toranomon Hills H ↗ Map
Occupying floors 47–52 of the original Toranomon Hills Mori Tower (2014), the Andaz is Hyatt's "anti-grand-hotel" concept executed brilliantly. Tony Chi and Shinichiro Ogata designed interiors using washi paper, rich wood, and floor-to-ceiling windows that make the 164 rooms feel like a well-appointed Tokyo apartment rather than a hotel. At 50 square meters, even the base rooms are enormous by Tokyo standards. The 52nd-floor rooftop — a first for a Tokyo high-rise hotel — includes an open-air bar and a space modeled on a traditional Japanese garden. This is the one for design-minded travelers who find the Ritz-Carlton too formal.
Hotel Toranomon Hills
Luxury Hotel World of Hyatt Toranomon · Toranomon Hills H ↗ Map
The newer, separate hotel from the Andaz — opened December 2023 inside the Toranomon Hills Station Tower. Danish studio Space Copenhagen designed all 205 rooms with Scandinavian-meets-Tokyo restraint: muted tones, natural materials, and six-meter ceilings in the lounge. The headline restaurant is Le Pristine Tokyo, a casual fine-dining spot from Michelin-starred Belgian chef Sergio Herman — his first Tokyo outpost. The 30 suites come with Japanese-style soaking tubs. A distinctly different proposition from the Andaz next door: younger, more Nordic, more food-forward.
Park Hyatt Tokyo
Luxury Hotel World of Hyatt Nishi-Shinjuku · Shinjuku EM ↗ Map
The Lost in Translation hotel, reopened December 2025 after a 19-month renovation by Paris-based Jouin Manku. Occupying floors 39–52 of Kenzo Tange's 1994 Shinjuku Park Tower, it now has 171 rooms with reimagined interiors heavy on marble and natural wood. The iconic New York Grill & Bar on the 52nd floor — where Bill Murray nursed whisky while a jazz combo played against the Shinjuku skyline — has been restored to its original spirit. Kozue, the Japanese restaurant, remains, and a new Girandole by Alain Ducasse replaces the old French spot. The pool with its vertiginous city views is still one of Tokyo's best hotel amenities.
ANA InterContinental Tokyo
Luxury Hotel IHG One Rewards Akasaka · Tameike-Sanno GN ↗ Map
The workhorse of Tokyo luxury hotels. Built in 1986 within Mori Building's pioneering Ark Hills complex — Japan's first large-scale private-sector urban redevelopment — it was rebranded to InterContinental in 2007. At 801 rooms it is by far the largest on this list, which means it handles big groups and conferences without breaking a sweat. The location between Akasaka and Roppongi is genuinely excellent for transit, with two metro stations under two minutes away. Suntory Hall, one of the world's great concert venues, is next door. It lacks the design cachet of the newer properties, but the rooftop pool is a summer highlight.
The Ritz-Carlton, Tokyo
Luxury Hotel Marriott Bonvoy Roppongi · Roppongi EH ↗ Map
Perched on floors 45–53 of Midtown Tower — Tokyo's tallest building when it opened in 2007 — the Ritz-Carlton delivers the most classically grand hotel experience on this list. The lobby on 45 is all black-and-cream marble with a towering bamboo installation, and many of the 245 rooms offer views of Mount Fuji on clear days. The recently renovated suites feature Nishijin brocade headboards, tatami flooring, and sliding shoji doors — proper Japanese craft, not theme-park japonisme. Tokyo Midtown itself houses the Suntory Museum of Art, 21_21 Design Sight, and serious shopping. The most traditionally luxurious option.
Trunk (Hotel)
Boutique Hotel Jingumae · Meiji-jingumae CF ↗ Map
Trunk opened in 2017 on Cat Street — the narrow, tree-lined backstreet that is the tastemaker spine of Harajuku — and immediately became the social hub Shibuya's creative class had been missing. Designed by Mount Fuji Architects Studio with interiors by Jamo Associates, the building is compact and angular, clad in reclaimed wood and recycled materials. The ground-floor lounge and terrace spill out onto Cat Street and function more like a neighborhood bar than a hotel lobby, packed on weekend evenings with a mix of guests and locals. With only 15 rooms, it operates at an intimate scale that is the polar opposite of the Otemachi tower hotels. The Trunk (Kitchen) restaurant and Trunk (Kushi) yakitori counter are destinations in their own right.
DDD Hotel
Design Hotel Bakurocho · Bakuro-Yokoyama S ↗ Map
The brainchild of the UDS collective — the same group behind MUJI Hotel Ginza — DDD channels that design-with-restraint sensibility into a far more affordable package. The ground-floor gallery-café EDIT doubles as both lobby and art space, with rotating exhibitions and genuinely good Allpress coffee. Rooms are compact but intelligently designed, with custom plywood furniture by Koichi Futatsumata, Marshall speakers, and curated bookshelves that feel like a well-traveled friend's apartment. It sits in the Bakurocho textile district, rapidly becoming one of Tokyo's most interesting neighborhoods — repurposed warehouse galleries and new-wave restaurants. If you care more about neighborhood character and design credibility than square footage, this is the best value in central Tokyo.
Tsuki Tokyo (月 東京)
Boutique Hotel Tsukiji · Tsukiji H ↗ Map
Tsuki leans hard into its Tsukiji location — the design nods to the area's maritime heritage with a restrained Japanese-modernist aesthetic of indigo aizome textiles, warm wood, and stone. Only around 30 rooms, which gives it a ryokan-like intimacy rare in central Tokyo. The real advantage is geography: steps from the outer market's remaining stalls and a short walk from Ginza, but the neighborhood still feels local and unhurried. Rooms are minimal but thoughtfully detailed, with hinoki-scented bath products and curated local snack selections. Operated by Tempo, a hospitality group focused on place-specific design — this hotel could not exist anywhere other than Tsukiji.
